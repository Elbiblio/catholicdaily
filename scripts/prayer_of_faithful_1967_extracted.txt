Page 4 PREFACE The Oratio Fidelium or Common Prayer of the People has been restored to the Mass by command of the Second Vatican Council. “Especially on these same days there is to be restored, after the Gospel with its homily, the Community Prayer or Prayer of the Faithful. By this prayer, in which the people are to take part, intercession will be made for Holy Church, for the civil authorities, for those oppressed by various needs, for all mankind, and for the salvation of the entire world.” — Constitution on the Sacred Liturgy art. 53. More detailed directions were given in the Instruction of September 1964, n. 56. “In places where the Community Prayer, or Prayer of the Faithful, is already an established custom, it is to take place before the Offertory, after the Oremus, and for the time being according to the formula already in use in the particular area. The celebrant may lead the prayer from the place where his seat is, from the altar, from an ambo, or from the altar-rails.” “The things to be prayed for, the invocations, may be chanted by a deacon or a cantor or some server able to do it, reserving to the celebrant the introductory words and the concluding prayer, which will ordinarily be : Deus, refugium nostrum et virtus (cf. Missale romanum, Orationes diversae, n. 20) or, on occasion, some other prayer which happens to be better suited to a particular need. “In places where the Community Prayer, or Prayer of the Faithful, is not yet customary, the competent regional authority may decree its introduction in the manner described above, using for the time being formulas approved by that authority itself.” The original selection prepared by the Liturgical Commission for England and Wales has been in use since July 1965 and has been generally welcomed. This fuller collection made for the Commission by Bishop Gordon Wheeler and Father David Quinlan gives a wider selection of invitations and prayers set out for each Sunday of the year. It should be noted that no particular selection of prayers is of obligation. The mind of the Church is to leave a wide freedom of choice of prayers appropriate to each occasion on which the Mass is celebrated. It is hoped that this augmented edition will help to this end. 25th July, 1966. * GEORGE PATRICK DWYER President of the National Liturgical Commission of England and Wales

Page 5 OF THE NATURE OF THE PRAYER OF THE FAITHFUL To understand the nature of the Oratio Fidelium, the style of its composition, and the manner in which it is to be said, the following points should be noted. (1) 1. It is a prayer of petition to God. Adoration and thanksgiving are not excluded, but this prayer is primarily asking for certain graces and favours. 2. The petitions are primarily for the intentions of the community , not of individuals. Hence the requests are for the whole Church, for all mankind, for various classes of people, e.g. the sick, and for the whole congregation here present. On certain occasions, however, e.g. Weddings, Ordinations, Funerals, it is appropriate for prayers to be added for the individual faithful concerned. 3. It is the prayer of the People. The congregation are not to be passive : they are the ones who are to say the prayer. Nor should they be content simply to say “Lord, graciously hear us.” Evidently a whole congregation cannot learn all these prayers by heart. But they should join in mind and heart with the intentions that are proposed. In composing this form of prayer for England and Wales it was therefore thought advisable (1) To frame the petitions as direct prayers to God so that the people could associate themselves with the words said and thus be actively praying, even though in silence. (2) (2) To include explicitly a space for silent prayer before the celebrant’s concluding prayer. It is much to be recommended that the reader should make a marked pause before saying “Lord, hear us” so that the people may be recollected and consciously link their response “Lord, graciously hear us” with the intention proposed. The Prayer of the Faithful is an integral part of the Mass. In the first part of the Mass, the Liturgy of the Word, the people have been reminded of the mighty works of God’s grace and of their vocation as Christians. The Prayer of the Faithful begs God to continue to give his grace so that all the People of God will live up to that vocation. OF THE STRUCTURE AND MINISTER OF THE PRAYER There are three elements in this prayer. 1. The Invitation to pray. The ancient Roman custom is that the Celebrant should say this, but the Instruction assigns it to the Deacon if present (n. 56). It may be omitted for a sufficient reason provided that the homily is based on the liturgy of the day. 2. The Petitions. These are normally five in number : (i) For the needs of the Universal Church; (ii) For the needs of the nation and of the human race at large; (iii) For classes of people who have special needs; (iv) For the Congregation and the local community; (v) The prayer to Our Lady inserted in the scheme authorized for England and Wales following a medieval precedent. It is constant and always concludes the petitions. It is recommended that these petitions be said by a Reader chosen from the congregation, who thus represents the People. In default of a Reader they are said by the Celebrant. On the occasions of weddings, funerals, and other special events, three further suitable petitions may be added after the first four. (The latter must always have a certain universal scope and should therefore be chosen from the categories specified above.) The maximum number of petitions on any occasion is eight. 3. Pause for silent prayer. 4. The final prayer : always said by the Celebrant.
